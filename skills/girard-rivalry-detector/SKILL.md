# Girard Rivalry Detector

---

## LAYER 1: INVARIANTS (Never override.)

1. **Rivalry is never self-reported.** Nobody admits to mimetic rivalry. The detection requires pattern-matching across behavioral signals — competitive framing, side-by-side comparisons, "look what I just did" posts aimed at peers, result screenshots. NEVER accept self-reported motivation as evidence of rivalry.

2. **Rivalry requires proximity.** Rivalry only forms between internal mediators — peers who occupy the same social sphere. Aspirational figures (celebrities, billionaires) do not trigger rivalry; they trigger admiration. Every rivalry cluster identified must demonstrate that the parties see each other as peers or near-peers.

3. **Contested objects require evidence of inflation.** A rivalry-inflated object is desired MORE because it is contested, not just because it has intrinsic value. Show the inflation evidence: the object is talked about in competitive frames, is referenced in "I need this before my competitor gets it" language, or generates disproportionate status signaling relative to its functional utility.

4. **Name specific people/handles.** Generic "entrepreneurial rivalry exists" is useless. The output must name specific individuals, handles, or named cohorts whose competitive dynamics are visible in public content. Every rivalry cluster must contain at least 3 named parties.

5. **Live research required.** All rivalry signals must come from current, fetchable public content. Training data is insufficient.

---

## LAYER 2: QUALITY GUIDANCE

6. **Focus on dense rivalries, not diffuse competition.** The entire market competes in a general sense. This tool finds the specific, tight rivalry clusters where competition is active, visible, and emotionally charged. Low-density competition (everyone vaguely competes) is not useful. High-density clusters (3-10 people visibly watching and competing with each other) are the prize.

7. **Map what they're competing FOR, not just with each other.** The contested object is the strategic opportunity. "They're competitive" is not actionable. "They're competing to be known as the most advanced AI operator in their peer group, and the contested objects are: (a) which AI tools they use, (b) how many team members they've replaced with AI agents, (c) whose results per hour are highest" — that is actionable.

8. **Rivalry-driven messaging is not manufactured rivalry.** The output should inform messaging that acknowledges and activates existing rivalry — not create fake competitive narratives. Distinguish between "naming a real rivalry" and "manufacturing a fake one."

---

## PURPOSE

Mimetic rivalry is the most intense form of desire — more powerful than aspiration, more durable than curiosity, more purchase-motivating than logic. When two peers both want the same object, the rivalry amplifies the desire for both parties beyond what the object would generate independently.

This tool maps those rivalries, identifies what objects are being contested, and shows how your product could position itself as the thing these rivals are competing to have.

**The entrepreneur market is drenched in mimetic rivalry.** Revenue screenshots, AI workflow comparisons, "who's ahead of whom" dynamics, tool adoption races — this is pure internal mediation. This tool makes the invisible rivalry structure visible and actionable.

## OUTPUT STRUCTURE: Rivalry Map

**Section 1: Active Rivalry Clusters**

For each cluster identified (target: 5-8 clusters):

- **Cluster name** — descriptive label (e.g., "Mid-6-Figure AI Operators", "Agency Owners Racing to AI-First")
- **Size** — approximate number of people in active competition
- **Named members** — at least 3 specific individuals/handles visible in this rivalry
- **What they're competing over** — the specific status markers, results, tools, or recognition at stake
- **Rivalry intensity** (1-10) — based on frequency of competitive signals in public content
- **Platform visibility** — where rivalry is most visible (Twitter/X threads, LinkedIn posts, podcast appearances, community forums)
- **Evidence** — 3+ specific public signals demonstrating rivalry behavior (competitive posts, comparison language, "I just did better than X" framing)

**Section 2: Contested Objects Inventory**

Cross-listing of what is being competed over across all clusters:

For each contested object:
- **Object** — the specific tool, credential, result, methodology, or recognition being competed for
- **Value inflation estimate** — how much more desired is this object because of rivalry vs. intrinsic value? (expressed narratively, not numerically)
- **Number of rivalry clusters contesting it** — more clusters = higher strategic value
- **Current "winner"** — if the rivalry has a current perceived leader, who is it?
- **Opportunity assessment** — could your product become associated with this contested object?

**Section 3: Positioning Opportunities**

For each opportunity where Rich's products could become a contested object:

- **Product fit** — which product (ZenithPro, Force Multiplier, Connect the Dots, etc.) is most relevant
- **Rivalry cluster** — which cluster would contest this product
- **Activation mechanism** — how to signal that having/completing this product has status in the rivalry context
- **Specific messaging angle** — language patterns drawn from the rivalry discourse that activate competitive desire without manufacturing fake rivalry

**Section 4: Rivalry-Driven Messaging Library**

10-15 specific language patterns extracted from the rivalry discourse that could inform marketing copy. These are not fabricated — they are patterns already present in how this market talks about competing with each other. Examples:
- "While others are still debating which AI tools to use, people in this program are already 6 months ahead"
- "The operators who figured this out first are now operating with a structural advantage that compounds"
- Specific phrasing patterns drawn from actual competitive posts in the market

**Section 5: Strategic Implications for Rich Schefren / Strategic Profits**

- Which rivalry clusters are most relevant to each product?
- Is Rich currently positioned inside any rivalry (as a contested object or as a model rivals compete to imitate)?
- What one rivalry dynamic, if named and activated explicitly, could drive the most near-term demand?

## RESEARCH PROCESS

### Step 1: Define the Target Market Cohort
Default: Online entrepreneurs $250K-$5M/year, AI-forward, sophisticated buyers.

### Step 2: Scan for Rivalry Signals

**Twitter/X:**
- Revenue screenshots and result comparisons in target market threads
- Quote-tweet competitive dynamics (people responding to each other's claims)
- "I just did X while others are still doing Y" post patterns
- Lists of "people to follow" that reveal who the market is watching

**LinkedIn:**
- "My results this month" posts with implicit competitor framing
- Posts that reference peers by name in achievement contexts
- Comments that reveal competitive awareness ("great work, but we just hit X too")

**Substack/Newsletter:**
- Newsletter vs. newsletter competitive framing
- "In contrast to what you're hearing from others..." positioning

**Community Forums (Skool, Circle, Facebook Groups, Reddit):**
- "What tools is everyone using?" threads that reveal competitive benchmarking
- "How do you compare to others at your level?" discussions
- Achievement-sharing threads that generate competitive responses

**Podcast Appearance Patterns:**
- Who gets booked on the same shows repeatedly (indicates shared rivalry space)
- How guests describe their peers vs. competitors

### Step 3: Cluster and Map
Group rivalry signals into clusters. Identify the contested objects. Rank by intensity and relevance to Rich's market.

### Step 4: Strategic Analysis
Apply findings to Rich's products and positioning.
