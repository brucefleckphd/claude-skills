# Girard Scapegoat Radar

---

## LAYER 1: INVARIANTS (Never override.)

1. **Scapegoating is a mechanism, not a sentiment.** Negative sentiment about a competitor is not a scapegoat cycle. A scapegoat cycle is characterized by: diffuse anxiety → convergence on a specific target → escalating collective blame → community bonding through shared opposition. All four stages must be present or clearly in progress for a finding to qualify as a scapegoat dynamic. Mere negativity is just negativity.

2. **Lifecycle stage must be assessed.** Every identified scapegoat gets a lifecycle classification: (a) Emerging — diffuse anxiety, target not yet clear; (b) Converging — market beginning to coalesce on a specific target; (c) Escalating — active campaign of collective blame; (d) Resolving — target has been "defeated" or community has moved on. Stage determines strategic urgency.

3. **Community cohesion signal required.** A scapegoat cycle produces a specific secondary signal: people who previously disagreed or ignored each other start agreeing and bonding around shared opposition. This cohesion signal distinguishes a scapegoat dynamic from ordinary competitive criticism. Look for unusual alliance formation, cross-ideological agreement, and "we can all agree on this" language.

4. **Named targets only.** A scapegoat must be identifiable — a specific person, platform, methodology, institution, or trend. "The establishment" is too vague. "Traditional business coaches who haven't adopted AI" is nameable and trackable.

5. **Live research required.** All findings must be grounded in current, fetchable public discourse. Training data is not sufficient for detecting active scapegoat cycles.

---

## LAYER 2: QUALITY GUIDANCE

6. **Early detection is the value.** The tool should identify scapegoat cycles while they're still emerging or converging — before they're obvious to the market. A cycle that's already at peak escalation is known by everyone; it's only valuable for positioning if it hasn't saturated yet. Weight earlier-stage cycles more heavily in strategic recommendations.

7. **Distinguish scapegoat from legitimate critique.** Not every negative sentiment is a scapegoat cycle. Legitimate critique targets specific behaviors with proportionate evidence. Scapegoating involves disproportionate blame, community bonding against the target, and anxiety displacement. Flag the distinction — both have strategic implications but different ones.

8. **Who is at risk of becoming a scapegoat?** This is often more valuable than who the current scapegoat is. If patterns suggest Rich's brand or category could become a scapegoat target, that's the highest-priority finding.

---

## PURPOSE

When a market experiences anxiety — about a technology shift, competitive pressure, economic uncertainty, or status disruption — it often resolves the anxiety by coalescing around a shared enemy. This is Girard's scapegoat mechanism: the community bonds through collective opposition to a target.

For marketers, this creates two strategic windows:
1. **Positioning as the alternative** — if your market is scapegoating "AI gurus who just teach tools," position as depth and strategy vs. tools
2. **Avoiding becoming the scapegoat** — if patterns suggest your category is building toward target status, reposition before the cycle escalates

**The entrepreneur/business market has particularly active scapegoat dynamics**: "fake gurus," "vanity metrics," "shiny object syndrome," "people who don't actually do the work" — these are recurring scapegoat targets that create community cohesion around opposition to them. The specific target rotates. The mechanism is constant.

## RESEARCH PROCESS

### Scan Sources for Scapegoat Signals

**Signal types to look for:**
- Increasing frequency of blame directed at a specific target across multiple voices
- Alliance formation between people who don't usually agree
- "Can we all agree that..." framing around a specific target
- Disproportionate emotional intensity relative to the actual offense
- Resolution or exodus language ("they should leave the space," "we should stop platforming them")

**Sources:**
- Twitter/X threads and quote-tweet chains in the target market
- LinkedIn comment sections where normally polite discourse gets emotional
- Reddit communities: r/entrepreneur, r/marketing, relevant niche subreddits
- Podcast hot takes and guest opinions
- Newsletter takes that name targets by name
- Community forum "calling out" threads
