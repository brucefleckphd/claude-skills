# Loewenstein Information Flow

---

## LAYER 1: INVARIANTS (Never override.)

1. **Map the buyer's knowledge state at every stage.** For each point in the funnel/journey, document: What does the buyer KNOW? What do they NOT KNOW? What do they THINK they know (that may be wrong)? What do they WANT to know? What are they AVOIDING knowing? This knowledge state determines whether curiosity, ostrich effect, or apathy operates at each point.

2. **The ostrich effect assessment is mandatory when the buyer's situation involves uncomfortable truths.** If the marketing is addressing a painful problem (business is failing, skills are outdated, competition is ahead, health is declining), the buyer may be actively AVOIDING the information the marketing is trying to deliver. The skill must assess: what truths is the buyer likely avoiding, and does the marketing confront those truths (triggering avoidance) or create a safe container for receiving them (enabling engagement)?

3. **Wanting/liking balance must be tracked across the full journey.** Marketing typically maximizes WANTING (desire, urgency, curiosity, FOMO). Delivery typically provides LIKING (value, insight, transformation). These are different neurological systems. The skill must assess whether the wanting generated by marketing is proportional to the liking delivered by the product. High wanting + low liking = unsustainable. Low wanting + high liking = invisible.

4. **Information that enables action is SOUGHT; information that only creates dissonance is AVOIDED.** This is the key variable for the ostrich effect. If the marketing makes uncomfortable information ACTIONABLE (here's the problem AND here's what you can do about it right now), it converts ostrich-avoidance into information-seeking. If the marketing just makes the buyer feel bad without an immediate path forward, it amplifies avoidance.

5. **Diversive vs. specific engagement must be distinguished at every content touchpoint.** Is the buyer engaging because they're genuinely progressing toward a decision (specific), or because the content is interesting-enough to consume passively (diversive)? High engagement metrics (time on page, email opens, video views) can mask diversive engagement that never converts. The skill must assess engagement quality, not just quantity.

---

## LAYER 2: QUALITY GUIDANCE

6. **The clickbait diagnostic.** Clickbait is the canonical wanting/liking mismatch: high wanting (curiosity gap), low liking (disappointing resolution). Apply this diagnostic to every information gap in the funnel. Is the resolution proportional to the gap? If not, the content is burning trust even while generating engagement.

7. **Information avoidance creates marketing barriers that proof can't overcome.** If the buyer is in ostrich mode (avoiding information about their real situation), more proof about the problem makes them avoid HARDER. The solution is to change the frame: lead with possibility rather than pain, make the diagnostic feel empowering rather than threatening, and ensure every uncomfortable truth comes packaged with an immediate action step.

8. **Post-purchase information flow is as important as pre-purchase.** The wanting/liking balance depends heavily on what information the buyer receives AFTER purchase: is onboarding re-selling (maintaining wanting) or delivering (creating liking)? Is the community creating social comparison that amplifies wanting, or providing support that increases liking? Map the full post-purchase information environment.

9. **The "knowledge curse" affects the client's own content design.** The client knows their subject deeply, which means they consistently OVERESTIMATE what their audience knows. This produces content that creates gaps the audience can't feel (because they don't have the foundational knowledge to perceive them) and resolves gaps the audience didn't know they had. The skill should surface this systematically.

## PURPOSE

Information is the medium through which all marketing operates — giving it, withholding it, revealing it, framing it. But most marketing treats information tactically (should we reveal the price? should we use a cliffhanger?). Loewenstein's work reveals the deeper architecture: information creates curiosity when it opens specific gaps, triggers avoidance when it threatens current beliefs, and produces satisfaction or disappointment based on the wanting/liking balance.

This skill audits the complete information architecture of a customer journey. It maps knowledge states, identifies ostrich-effect barriers, evaluates the wanting/liking balance, classifies engagement quality, and produces recommendations for designing information flows that drive action AND satisfaction.

## EXECUTION PROCESS

### Step 1: Knowledge State Mapping

For each stage of the journey, document the buyer's information environment:
- **Knows:** Confirmed knowledge at this point
- **Doesn't know:** Gaps — some they're aware of (curiosity), some they're not (latent)
- **Thinks they know:** Possible misconceptions that will affect receptivity
- **Wants to know:** Active information-seeking motivations
- **Avoiding knowing:** Ostrich-effect zones — truths they're steering away from
- **Just learned:** New information delivered at this stage

### Step 2: Ostrich Effect Assessment

For the specific audience and topic:
- What uncomfortable truths does the marketing need to communicate?
- Which of those truths is the buyer likely to avoid?
- How does the marketing handle those truths: confrontation (triggers avoidance), safe container (enables reception), or avoidance (never addresses them)?
- For each ostrich zone: is the information made ACTIONABLE (converts avoidance to seeking)?
- Overall ostrich severity: how much of the audience is in active avoidance about the core problem?

### Step 3: Wanting/Liking Balance Audit

Map the two systems across the journey:
- **Wanting curve:** How intense is acquisition motivation at each stage? What's driving it (curiosity, urgency, FOMO, aspiration, mimetic desire)?
- **Liking curve:** How much genuine satisfaction/value does the buyer experience at each stage? What's producing it (insight, results, community, recognition)?
- **Gap analysis:** Where does wanting significantly exceed liking? (unsustainable — burns trust). Where does liking exceed wanting? (invisible — great product nobody tells anyone about)
- **Post-purchase crossover:** After purchase, does wanting DECLINE and liking INCREASE (healthy)? Or does wanting decline and liking also decline (dangerous)?

### Step 4: Engagement Quality Classification

For every content touchpoint:
- Is engagement diversive (passive consumption, general interest) or specific (directed toward decision/action)?
- What percentage of engagement is converting to the next stage?
- Are there content pieces that generate high engagement but zero progression? (entertainment, not marketing)
- Are there content pieces with low engagement but high progression? (high-quality, poorly surfaced)

### Step 5: Information Architecture Assessment

Evaluate the overall design:
- **Revelation pacing:** Is information revealed in a sequence that builds curiosity and sustains action, or dumped all at once?
- **Gap chain:** Does each resolved gap open a new one, creating a chain that pulls the buyer forward?
- **Actionability density:** At every point where the buyer receives information, can they DO something with it?
- **Resolution proportionality:** Does the value of each resolution match the intensity of the gap that created it?

### Step 6: Recommendations

Produce specific architectural recommendations:
- Where to convert diversive engagement to specific engagement
- How to address ostrich-effect barriers without triggering deeper avoidance
- Where to rebalance wanting and liking
- How to improve resolution proportionality
- Where to add actionability to information that's currently just diagnostic
- How to redesign the post-purchase information flow for sustained liking

### Step 7: Self-Examination

Surface the client's information-architecture blind spots:
- Are they creating content that entertains but doesn't convert? (diversive trap)
- Are they assuming the audience knows more than they do? (knowledge curse)
- Are they optimizing for wanting metrics (opens, clicks, views) at the expense of liking metrics (completion, satisfaction, referral)?
- Are they avoiding the uncomfortable truths their audience needs to hear? (client-side ostrich effect)
